Introduction

Bark Hint is a project that began as a class homework assignment aimed at demonstrating our individual design skills. I identified and explored a problem space, designed a solution, and developed a low-fidelity prototype.

Background - Problem Space

People, as individuals, are busy and forgetful. Families, large and small, are often not at home at the same time. Chores are forgotten, schedules are miscommunicated. There is a plethora of smartphone apps to facilitate communication, but most, if not all, require significant setup and are not situationally aware. The phone might know it’s at home via GPS or WiFi, but unless there is Bluetooth in every room, it only knows that it’s at home and nothing more specific. There are many tasks that one needs to be reminded of: starting, running, unloading both the dishwasher and the washer/dryer; folding clothes; prepping dinner, making snacks, cleaning the kitchen; taking out the trash; etc.

A specific issue involves other beings in the home that can’t communicate with us via speech or text, at least not yet. Often pets are accidentally neglected, even if for a relatively short period of time. For example, one morning, someone forgot to feed the dog, or all parties involved assumed the other party was responsible for the task. Other things the dog might need, but might be forgotten by the human, include a trip outside for relief or their medication. An individual dog owner might forget, families might collectively forget or miscommunicate as discussed, and then there are other caretakers like friends or dog sitters. How can the relevant parties communicate, without the need to constantly send text messages or download another smartphone app? What gentle reminders, within the home, can be given to the human to provide care?

For some of these tasks, solutions already exist. To feed the dog, there are automatic pet feeders which can release food via a smartphone. However, this removes the human-dog interaction, which, hopefully, is the reason the dog was adopted in the first place. Rather than removing this interaction, the owner should be gently reminded that their dog is probably hungry. Trained dogs might be able to ring a bell to go outside, but not all dogs can. And this doesn’t provide a solution if there is no one home and whoever left last forgot to give the dog a chance to go outside. Finally, there are automatic pill dispensers for humans that could likely be appropriated for pets, but again, it removes the human-dog interaction and would also only work if it were a tasty pill.

Task

The task, from the user perspective, is to take care of their dog at the appropriate time. This includes 1) remembering the needs of their dog, at the appropriate time, and 2) attending to their needs. “Appropriate” time means the time at which the dog needs the care and the user is available to give it. The user should also be able to find out if their dog’s needs have been attended to by another involved person. The task, from the system perspective, is to automatically recognize and display to the human the current state of the dog’s needs. Again, these needs include taking the dog out, feeding the dog, and giving the dog any necessary medication.

Users

The users of this system are anyone responsible for the care of a dog (or dogs) in a household. This leads to a broad range of user characteristics: from children, if given responsibility by parents, to older adults. It might be a single individual, a family of four, or an extended family of 10 all living together. It will be assumed the users are responsible and generally enthusiastic about their dog, but possibly forgetful. Not all users are “tech savvy”, since “dog owners” encompasses such a range of people.

Implications

  • The system should encourage interaction with the dog. (Because the users love their dog but can be forgetful).
  • The system should inform the user on whether or not the dog’s needs have been attended to. (Because there might be miscommunication among household members).
  • The system should provide this information as a gentle reminder, not in an accusatory manner. (Because the users love their dog and thus will respond to a helpful reminder).
  • The system should not require additional steps to obtain this information. (Because the users are busy and need to act quickly).
  • The system should be integrated with household items or the general household environment. (Because the system will be situated in the dog’s home).
  • The system should be aware of the dog’s ideal patterns of care, like when he should be fed, taken out, given medication. (Because the user needs to informed at the appropriate time).

Solution

To take advantage of the household environment, the design will focus on feedback within the items associated with each need of the dog. There will be consistent visual cues using color tied with each item. Green will mean the task has been taken care of, yellow will signify that is around the time the task is typically completed, and blue will represent that it has been at least an hour since the time the task is typically completed. Green typically signifies “OK”, yellow is a bright, visible color that grabs attention, and blue is not accusatory like red, and it can be interpreted as “sad” because the dog hasn’t been attended to. The task the user does for all of these solutions is attending to the dog at the appropriate time. By feeding, taking out, and giving medication to the dog, the user is completing the task, and the system responds with appropriate feedback.

1. Feeding: The dog bowl will change colors based on the time and completion of feeding.

Example: If the owner(s) feed the dog around 8 a.m. and then again around 6 p.m., the bowl will show yellow from 7:30 a.m. to 8:30 a.m., or until the task is completed. If the task is not completed by 8:30 a.m., it will show blue until the task is completed. Once the task is completed, it will glow green until 5:30 p.m., when it will turn yellow and repeat the pattern again.






2. Walking or Going Out: Rather than attaching something to the leash, which might be in the way while actually using it, the hook (or its mount) for the leash will change colors. Removing the leash does not necessarily signify that the dog is going on a walk or getting exercise. However, we are assuming it means the dog is “out” and most good dog owners let dogs go potty while “out”.

Example: If the owner normally takes the dog out every six hours, then from the time that the dog returns from outside up until five and half hours later, the hook will be green. From five and a half hours from the last time out to six and a half hours from the last time out, the hook will be yellow. Anytime past six and a half hours from the last time out, the hook will be blue.


3. Medicine: Because medicine might be in liquid, pill, or treat form, a special storage unit that contains the medicine will act as the visual cue.

Example: If the dog needs to have a pill mixed in with a treat at 9 p.m., then the box will remain green until 8:45 p.m. at which point it will turn and remain yellow until 9:15 p.m. Afterwards, it will be blue until the medication is taken. A shorter time interval exists for this device because timely medication is likely more important than timely going out or feeding. With any digital product, there is some sort of setup. The devices will be connected via WiFi, and variables like time setup online. This separate interface is given less attention since it is infrequently used.



Bringing it all together: In addition to the responsive objects, a “task” chart that is aesthetically pleasing will be available to hang on a wall. Users can choose whether or not to use this in addition to the objects. It can be placed in a room that is frequently used or an area that is passed by often. This would be appropriate if the items (food bowl, leash, medication box) are typically kept out of sight. If the user chooses, the chart can make an unobtrusive sound when the color changes from green to yellow, halfway through the yellow period, and then yellow to blue. This would be appropriate for situations where the user sees the color change, then promptly forgets again; as well as those who are visually impaired. The auditory cue can be decoupled from the “task” chart and used as a standalone speaker output. Having visual cues, and minimal auditory cues, stems from design implications like the user should be gently reminded in a non-accusatory manner, and the user doesn’t have to take any additional steps - the information is automatic and always present.

Prototype

I created a quick prototype of the dog bowl, using an existing dog bowl (why make a new one when it already exists, at this stage?) and 3 LEDs. A force-sensitive resistor attached to the bottom of the bowl detects change in weight. For purposes of the prototype, I manually set the LEDs to change quickly in order to show what would happen over a longer period of time.

Next I will solder the parts together and design a dog bowl to 3D print. For a higher-fidelity prototype, I need a custom dog bowl with a paw print to light up with LEDs, and housing underneath for the hardware.

In theory, this system could be expanded as activity recognition technologies grow.
